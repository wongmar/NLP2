Transformers Applications

Introduction

"He who learns a lot but applies a little stays little.", Amit Kalantri

Roberts (2019) designed a transformer meta-model based on the simple assertion that,

Every NLP problem can be represented as a text-to-text function. Every type of NLP task provides some kind of text context that generates some form of text response. A text-to-text representation of any NLP task provides a unique framework to analyze transformers' methodology and practice. The idea is for a transformer to learn a language through transfer learning during the training and fine-tuning phases with a text-to-text approach.

This model is named as T5 from the phrase a Text-To-Text Transfer Transformer.

Designing a universal text-to-text model utilized first the NLP technical revolution started with Vaswani (2017), the original Transformer. The second progress towards a magical solution was OpenAI GPT-2 and GPT-3 Models focusing taking transformer models from fine-tuning pretrained models to few-shot trained models that required no fine-tuning. Boldly, the second step was to show that a machine can learn a language and apply it to downstream tasks as we humans do.

Humans learn a language and then apply that knowledge to a wide range of NLP tasks through transfer learning. The core concept of a T5 model is to find an abstract model that can do things like us. When humans communicate, we always start with a sequence that is followed by another sequence which in turn, becomes the start sequence leading to another. Similarly, music also is composed of organized sounds, dancing with organized body movements, painting with coordinated shapes and colors, and finally language with a word or a group of words we call text. Understanding text requires attention to all of the words in the sentence in all directions, measuring the importance of each term. If a sentence is confusing, we focus on a word and query the rest of the keywords in the sentence to determine their values and the attention we must pay to them. This defines the attention layers of transformers.

Reading comprehension requires many skills applied all at once. Reading a text involves parsing the keywords and the main events, and then creating some kind of representations of the content, probably built on past experience and knowledge. Summarization can be done by applying text-to-text transformations. Questions can be answered using the knowledge of the content and the representations built. In addition, each question is examined to avoid traps and making mistakes.

Sentiment Analysis

The principle of compositionality is the paradigm that the meaning of a complex expression is determined by the meanings of its ingredient expressions and the rules used to combine them. Sentiment analysis relies on the principle of compositionality. Understanding parts of a sentence helps understanding a whole sentence. NLP transformer models can accomplish the user emotion detection task via various models as in below sections.

Questions and Answers

Transformers are powerful state-of-the-art models but still they cannot answer open questions easily. An open environment means that somebody can ask any question on any topic, and a transformer would answer correctly. That is still impossible to accomplish. If they could, perhaps a Turing test could be beaten?

Transformers often use general domain training datasets in a closed question-and-answer environment. For example, critical answers in medical care and law interpretation require additional NLP functionality. However, transformers cannot answer any question correctly regardless of whether the training environment is closed with preprocessed question-answer sequences or not. If a sequence contains more than one subject and compound propositions, a transformer model can make wrong predictions.

Below, a DistilBERT model is used to answer questions by introducing Named Entity Recognition (NER) functions that suggest reasonable questions.

def summarize(_model, _tokenizer, _text, _maxlen):
    preprocess_text = _text.strip().replace('\n',' ')
    t5_prepared_text = "summarize: " + preprocess_text
    print("Preprocessed and prepared text:\n", t5_prepared_text)

    tokenized_text = _tokenizer.encode(t5_prepared_text, return_tensors="pt").to(device)

    # summmarize
    summary_ids = _model.generate(tokenized_text,
                                  num_beams=4,
                                  no_repeat_ngram_size=2,
                                  min_length=30,
                                  max_length=_maxlen,
                                  early_stopping=True)

    output = _tokenizer.decode(summary_ids[0], skip_special_tokens=True)
    return output
